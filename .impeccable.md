## Design Context

### Users
Data engineers, university professors reviewing capstone submissions, and technically-minded students. They use this dashboard in a lab or office setting — likely at a desk, on a large monitor, in low ambient light. The primary job is: "Show me that this pipeline is real and the data is credible."

### Brand Personality
**Precise. Measured. Unimpressed.**
This is a data instrument, not a marketing page. Think of the feeling of a Bloomberg terminal or a well-calibrated scientific instrument — information-dense, typographically rigorous, completely free of decoration that doesn't earn its place.

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Theme**: Dark. Always dark. SREs, data engineers, and pilots use dark interfaces because they're staring at them for hours in darker environments.
- **NOT**: Cyan-on-dark glow effects, gradient text, neon accents, rounded glassmorphism cards, emoji-heavy interfaces, or anything that reads as "portfolio demo."
- **References**: NASA Mission Control displays, financial terminal UIs, Grafana in dark mode, The Guardian's data journalism pages.
- **Typography**: A geometric monospace for data labels/numbers (Geist Mono or Azeret Mono), paired with a clean, slightly-condensed sans for labels and headings (Barlow or Jost). NOT Inter. NOT Syne. NOT Space Grotesk.
- **Color**: Deep navy base (oklch ~15% L, hue 240), warm amber accent for primary metric highlights (oklch ~75% L, hue 55), soft slate for secondary text. City accent colors should be perceptually distinct but not neon: a muted teal (Mumbai), soft amber (Delhi), sage green (Bangalore).
- **Zero emojis**: Replace all emoji with SVG inline icons or text labels. Emojis break the instrument aesthetic entirely.

### Design Principles
1. **Data first**: Every visual element must earn its space by serving the data. Remove anything decorative that doesn't encode information.
2. **Typographic hierarchy over iconography**: Use font weight and size to establish importance, not icons and colors.
3. **Functional restraint**: No rounded cards everywhere, no glassmorphism, no box-shadows at 32px blur. Use thin rules, muted backgrounds, precise borders only where needed.
4. **Precision language**: Labels should be exact and unit-aware. "38.0 C" not "38.0°C feels like 40°C Haze". Numbers are the hero.
5. **Impeccable status signaling**: Pipeline health and data quality should be immediately readable at a glance — not buried in tables.
